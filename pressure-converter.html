Pressure Units and Their History

Pressure is the force exerted per unit area and is measured using various units. Over the years, different cultures and fields have used different units to measure pressure. Today, the most widely used system of pressure units is the International System of Units (SI).

The Pascal (Pa)

The SI unit of pressure is the Pascal (Pa), named after the French mathematician, physicist, and philosopher Blaise Pascal. The Pascal is defined as one newton per square meter (N/m²). This unit is used in a wide range of scientific and engineering applications, such as in fluid mechanics, atmospheric science, and material science.

The Bar (bar)

Another common unit of pressure is the bar (bar), which is a metric unit of pressure equal to 100,000 Pascals or 1,000 millibars. The bar is commonly used in the measurement of atmospheric pressure and is often used in weather reports and aviation.

Pounds per Square Inch (psi)

In the United States, the most commonly used unit of pressure is pounds per square inch (psi). The psi is defined as the pressure exerted by a force of one pound-force applied to an area of one square inch. This unit is commonly used in the automotive and construction industries.

The Atmosphere (atm)

The atmosphere (atm) is another unit of pressure commonly used in meteorology and the study of gases. It is defined as the pressure exerted by the weight of the Earth's atmosphere at sea level. One atmosphere is equivalent to 101,325 Pascals or 14.7 psi.

The Torr (Torr)

Historically, the Torr (Torr) was a common unit of pressure used in vacuum measurements. It is named after the Italian physicist and mathematician Evangelista Torricelli, who invented the mercury barometer in the 17th century. One Torr is equivalent to 1/760th of an atmosphere.

Units of Pressure in Medicine

In medicine, two common units of pressure are millimeters of mercury (mmHg) and centimeters of water (cmH2O). The mmHg is a unit of pressure equal to the pressure exerted by a column of mercury 1 millimeter high at the Earth's surface. The cmH2O is a unit of pressure equal to the pressure exerted by a column of water 1 centimeter high at 4°C.

The Inch of Mercury (inHg)

Finally, the inch of mercury (inHg) is a unit of pressure commonly used in the United States and aviation. It is equal to the pressure exerted by a column of mercury 1 inch high at the Earth's surface. One inHg is equivalent to 3,386.39 Pascals or 0.491 psi.

Conversions Between Pressure Units

Conversions between these units of pressure can be made using conversion factors. For example, to convert from Pascals to psi, one would divide the pressure in Pascals by 6,894.76. To convert from psi to bar, one would multiply the pressure in psi by 0.0689476. Conversions between other units can be made using similar conversion factors.
